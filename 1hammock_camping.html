Backpacking Gear

Hammock Checklist

Overall Information:

I am going to recommend you look through shugemery's YouTube channel for different hammock reviews.
This guy gives great information and shows off different hammocks with a lot of know-how.
He has different playlists for reviews, hammocks, types of hammocks, and so much more. Plus he's funny.

Backpacking Hammock:

My hammock choice brand is Warbonnet Outdoors.
My hammock is the Blackbird XLC series.
I payed for the lightweight double layered because I read that if you are sleeping without a sleeping bag and you have a single layered hammock that mosquitoes can bite through your hammock.
I also think I got a 'headbox' or something for mine, it might just come with the hammock but it is a storage area inside the hammock. I can't imagine not having mine.
Again shugemery has a lot of information on different brands and hammocks so go check it out if warbonnet is too expensive or just not for you.

No matter what hammock you get, you should find tree straps for your suspension option as they are less harmful to trees and just as durable as any other option.

Tarp / Cover:

I also got my tarp from Warbonnet Outdoors.
My tarp is the thunderfly, which is pretty pricey for a tarp.
The reason I purchased such an expensive tarp is because when you buy backpacking gear (as opposed to camping gear) you are paying for long lasting but lightweight material.
A cheaper tarp will add MUCH more weight to your pack and again you are welcome to come check out my gear and see for yourself how light it is.
As long as you get a hammock tarp designed for backpacking it doesn't really matter what you get.
I decided to splurge on a more expensive one because of how much I knew I would use it.

Underquilt:

Honestly, unless you are camping in winter weather; the underquilt you choose doesn't matter too much.
You do need to have one!!! But I bought a Wise Owl brand, cheapest that I could find, underquilt that does the trick.
If you buy cheap, it is going to be heavier and for backpacking the lighter your stuff is the easier your hikes will be.
If you have the money it is worth buying a down underquilt, down is way lighter, warms better, breathes easier if it's to hot, blah blah...
The Warbonnet Wookie quilt matches with the hammock perfectly and is very lightweight and perfect but quite pricy.

For temperature rating make sure you read my sleeping bag info on the recommended page.
Anything around 20°F or 0°C should be good for most temperatures and screw winter camping.
