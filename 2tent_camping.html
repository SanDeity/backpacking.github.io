Backpacking Gear

Tent Checklist

Tent:

Sharing a tent with another person can reduce overall group weight and keep each other a bit warmer.
You can also look into tents that use your own hiking poles as the supports.
The tent Ben borrowed worked like that and takes maybe a minute max to set up.

I don't have a lot of information on tents since I move to hammock camping many years ago.
Just keep in mind you are looking for either a 1 person or two person BACKPACKING tent.
shugemery might have some info on tents as well, but I really only know his hammock content.
I also recommend browsing different reddit posts when looking for a backpacking tent, people share a lot of good info there that you won't see in reviews or on the website. This is a fairly cheap "2 person" tent that Ben borrowed from Jason.
Using hiking poles to prop up the tent is convienient and easy to set up, and you don't have to carry poles.
I would probably buy this tent if I weren't above the ground-sleeping peasants.

Sleeping Pad:

This is an important one. A lot of ultralight backpackers use the fold up sleeping pads.
I don't know what the heck is wrong with them but it's just not worth destroying your back when you can sleep on an inflatable pad.
This is one of the areas that you should carry a little extra weight to make your trip more enjoyable (like bringing a hammock setup)
If you can sleep happily on the ground with just a foam pad, more power to you I guess.

Static V by Klymit is an excellent, durable pad for backpacking.
This is what both Carter and Ben borrowed for our trip.
I don't know of their other sleeping pad products but an insulated static V should be all you ever need for a tent backpacking sleeping pad.
If you want to go a less quality but cheaper route any inflatable sleeping pad marketed for backpacking would be fine.
