Backpacking Gear

BOOTS (and cats):

This is maybe the most important thing to spend big money on.
There are a bunch of good brands out there, if you can try on boots at some place keep an eye out for Oboz, Merrell, and Kuru.
They say a pound on your feet is like 5 on your back, so a lot of people buy lightweight shoes.

I LOVE Oboz brand boots.
I've worn mine for over 5 years, as everyday footwear and hiking boots.
The same pair I wear over to a friends house today is the same pair I took to Hawaii idk how many years ago.
Yeah I probably need a new pair by now but it's amazing they have lasted this long.
Also I should mention when you get them and if you treat them every year or so, they are virtually waterproof.
My mother slipped and submerged her entire foot, but her feet stayed dry.

Kuru is a pretty good brand of shoes as well, but I have not worn any.

Backpack:

Really the only thing to do for a good hiking bag is make a trip to REI or similiar hiking specific store where an employee can fit you for a bag and you can load it up and walk around the store with it.
Everyone seems to have their own preferences when it comes to bags but all that really matters is you get a real bag that fits you, not some cheap walmart one that doesn't have the correct supports or whatever.
A real bag will have it's own water protective cover and adjust to fit your size (not every bag adjusts to all sizes though).
It might also be worthwile to bring your tent and or sleeping setup to make sure they fit in the bag, although as long as you don't get a tiny ultralight bag most should fit your sleep setup.

Sleeping Bag:

Look for any backpacking sleeping bag that fits your dimensions.
When it comes to rating, I don't recommend you buy the lowest temperature possible, and the same goes for your sleeping bag.
Anything around 0°C - 30°C for comfort level should be fine (if it doesn't specify look for -10°C as the lower number).
My bag is rated for comfort: 5°C - 40°C, and for extreme temp -15°C - 5°C.
If you ever plan for winter camping, you basically need mostly new gear anyway so I don't recommend trying to use the same bag for both.

Apparel:

For backpacking apparel it is also up to your own preference.
The most common way to pack is to bring 1 good hiking shirt, 1 good pair of hiking pants, and then multiple base layers of clothing (socks, underwear, underarmour and the such).
Trial and error for your preferred clothing.

More important is a good light weight rain jacket (columbia brand maybe), and a down puffer for night time.
My down puffer packs neatly into a small bag and fits inside any side pocket of my bag which is really convienient.
Frequently it is much colder at night than expected so it's just smart to have an extra lightweight down jacket to keep you alive.

Gotta have a hiking hat too, necessary for long hikes in the sun.

Other:

Quickfire other things you should own.

Water filtration system. Sawyer filters work fine and are durable, although a hand pump is just so much easier to use. Quality of life upgrade.

Get a head lamp.

A backpacking knife is pretty useful

There are tons of cooking options, but all you should ever need is a multi-utensil and a Jetboil.
The Jetboil has a range of products and most work fine but ideally you should get one with the stove attachtment and that packs in neatly.

Most hikers insist you own a GPS system, although is is quite pricy.
Along with navigation, a firestarter, a backpacking first aid kit, and tent patch kit can save your trip or even your life.
Most first aid kits also have an emergency foil blanket, if they don't you might consider adding one.

This is basically everything you NEED to bring on a backpacking trip.
