Hello gamers, this is a comprehensive list of my personal recommendations and a checklist for you to begin your backpacking lifestyle.

This link will take you to a basic checklist and is what I start with when I begin packing for a trip.


IMPORTANT NOTE:
Everything that you look to buy is going to be more expensive when you purchase the 'backpacking' version.
This is because backpacking items are going to be much lighter weight and more durable.
You are paying a premium for long lasting, light weight, quality supplies.
You may choose to go the cheaper route on some items but every extra pound makes the trip MUCH harder and it really adds up.
The rule of thumb is 20% of your body weight in your pack. Many people take weighing packs very seriously and every pound you can eliminate honestly helps a lot.

Hammock VS Tent Case Study

Here's the rundown pal: Hammock backpacking is superior in pretty much every way. I'll make a little pro / con list for you though.

Pros:
Hammock isn't on the ground - consistantly comfortable sleeping EVERY TIME.
It's worth mentioning twice because a bad night's rest can ruin a trip: COMFORT!
No tracking dirt into the tent, or having to remove your shoes to look through your stuff
Flooding is a non-issue. PERIOD.
You look cooler than all the peasant ground sleepers.

Cons:
Setting up a hammock can take longer, but obviously it's worth it. You remember hanging out under my tarp. That was cool.
It is easier to stay warm without open air below ya bum.
I'm not sure the safety a tent OR a hammock provides when camping in bear country, but a tent does FEEL safer...


Side notes: Some say that a hammock is more susceptible to the weather. The truth is that tent and hammocks face different challanges with the weather.
For example, with proper knot tying; my hammock and tarp withstood 'tornado' level winds that collapsed Jason's tent in a downpour.
Who can say whether it was the wind or rains, but they had to take shelter in my parents tent for the night as theirs collapsed and flooded. I however was safe and sound.
The struggles a hammock faces are that everything will be colder, since you have ventilation underneith as well as above.
With a properly rated sleeping bag and underquilt, this is no issue.
We were both pretty cold at our last trip but it was pretty manageable for Juno and I. I can't speak for you though.
Also being cooler is a plus when camping in summer!

My final argument for hammocks: If weight or comfort is your concern, there are a wide range of hammocks for different weight ranges and heights.
It's also important to note that an everyday hammock is not designed for sleeping in, and does not represent real hammocks.
They are only about 8-9 feet long and don't have a ridgeline.
My hammock is 11 feet long and is designed for people up to 6'6'', with a maximum weight rating of 350lbs.
You are MORE than welcome to come over sometime and lie around in my hammock if you are considering getting one.


Regardless which you choose, I have recommendations for both. A hammock setup would cost more but there are a lot of factors in play.
